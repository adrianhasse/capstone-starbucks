Project Definition

Project Overview

This project is the final capstone projects of the Data Scientist Nanodegree of Udacity provided by Starbucks. The project includes simulated data that mimics customer behavior on the Starbucks rewards mobile app. The data includes information about potential offers, customers and transactions. This allows further analysis of a specific customer group in particular how they interact with offers and incentives.

The data is made available in 3 separate json files:

  • portfolio.json — contains all potential offers of Starbucks
  • profile.json — contains further information of the participating customers
  • transcript.json — contains interactions between Starbucks and customers

There are three types of offers referring to either an advertisement, a discount or a BOGO (buy one get one) offer. For each offer, except the advertisement, the customer has to spent a certain amount to get the benefit which is either a discount or an additional beverage. The profiles of each customer provides additional information about the customers like gender, income or the time when they first registered in the app. The largest data set is called transcript and contains all transactions between the customers and Starbucks including the corresponding time, date and amount as well as their interactions with offers. Whenever a customer receives, views or completes an offer the date, time and offer id is stored.

Problem Statement

The question of how likely it is that a particular offer will be completed is now further analyzed. In other words, when a customer receives an offer, how likely is it that it will be completed and he or she receives either a discount or an additional beverage.

This is a rather difficult problem to solve because the data contains a fair amount of noise. For example is it possible that a customer completes an offer without even knowing about the offer. This is the case when no information channel like an app or email reaches the customer. Thus, sending out an offer and completing the same one have often no causal relation.

The above formulated question is tackled as regression problem since it is more flexible and also delivers more information compared to a simple binary classification problem. The chosen model then provides not only what it considers more likely in the form of 0 or 1, but also a measure of how confident it actually is.

To solve the regression problem, a random forest model is used which generalizes quite well and also offers the possibility to take non-linear relationships into account. The model’s hyperparameter are then further optimized using cross validation. To improve the solution quality further, additional features will be engineered, which will be described later in more detail.

Since the data is simulated and contains quite some noise a fairly accurate model is expected, especially considering the small amount of features. Nevertheless the model should be good enough to give an advantage in practice.

Metrics

For generating the trees of the random forest the mean squared error measure is used. It measures the quality of the model’s predictions by calculating the average of the squared difference between the actual values and their predictions. In general, the lower the mean squared error, the better the model quality. For each split the feature with the corresponding value is chosen that returns the lowest mean squared error.

This measure has the advantage that it performs very well for a regression and since there are by definition no outliers in the data, its biggest disadvantage is of the table.

To measure the final quality of the model the coefficient of determination R² is used.

The closer R² is to 1 the better the quality of the predictions. Basically the final model’s mean squared error is compared to the mean squared error of the most simple model which is predict always the mean. If R² is around 0 both models have about the same quality, if it is negative the simple model predicts better and if R² is positive the final model’s predictions are on average closer the the actual values.

Analysis

Data Exploration and Visualization

There are in total 10 offers which refer to either an advertisement, a discount or a BOGO (buy one get one) offer. Each offer can be distributed via web, email, mobile or social channels although not all channels are available for each offer. Each offer remains valid for a certain period of time, which is not more than 10 days.

The data contains 17000 customers of all ages (from 18 till 100) with a reasonable distribution. Also, the income of over 87 % of the customers is available. The pictures below show the age and income distribution.

In total the data contains 306534 events which refer to ‘offer received’, ‘offer viewed’, ‘offer completed’ and ‘transaction’. Furthermore, a customer can receive several different offers simultaneously. Therefore, they may overlap in time which has to be consider for further data preparation. In addition, offers that are ‘informational’, which means that they are just advertisements, cannot be completed.

Most customers spent on average between 1 and 5 units of money which corresponds to buying a single beverage. This seems reasonable for Starbucks.

Methodology

Data Preprocessing

Since the data is simulated not much data cleaning is necessary. The following histogram shows one of the exceptions. If an age of a customer is not available the age of 118 is assumed. This was corrected by replacing it with the mean.

In general, missing values refer mostly to categorical variables like ‘gender’. They were treated as a separated category.

Implementation

In order to find out which offers were completed , one has to iterate over all events. This is also helpful for further feature engineering. The data set was therefore reduced to 61042 data points, all referring to offers of the type BOGO (buy one get one) and discount. From these offers 33101 were completed.

I calculated 3 additional features:

  • The average amount spent on each transaction,
  • the average amount spent over time and
  • the average time taken to recognize an offer.

To prevent data leakage, all three features always incorporate the date and time of the corresponding offer. This means, for example, that only transactions that took place before the date and time of the offer were used to calculate the average amount spent on each transaction of a particular offer.

The final data set containing 61042 data points was split into a training set, a test set and a validation set. The training set contains 75 % of the data points, the test set 18.75 % and the validation set 6.25 %. This gives us enough data point for training the model while still leaving more than 3,800 data point for the final validating process and robustness test.

Refinement

Choosing a random forest as regression model, cross-validation was used to tune hyperparameters. Especially the minimum number of samples in a leaf turned out to increase the model quality and performance. This parameter prevents the tree from further splitting up a tree by demanding a certain minimum amount of remaining data points. This prevents the model from over-fitting but also increase the calculation speed since less splittings have to be performed.

Using cross-validation to further adjust the random forest model a coefficient of determination R² of around 35 % was achieved in the final validation set. This is not no bad considering that there were not to many features available.

Results

Model Evaluation and Validation

The final outcome of the model lead to a a coefficient of determination R² of 36 % for the test set and 35 % for the validation set. Although a cross-validation was performed, the model seems to be very robust, since the mean squared error is not significantly different.

To gather further information concerning the features, an additional feature importance analysis was performed. It turned out, that the the three engineered feature increased the model quality drastically shown the the bar chart below.

In particular the average amount spent for each transaction added improved the quality a lot.

Justification

The final results meet expectations, especially considering the discussed noise contained in the data. The model could certainly be used for a better predicting if an offer will be successful. Also, the model seems quite robust comparing the R² of the training set, test set and validation set. Especially the feature engineering increased the model’s quality drastically.

Conclusion

The project focuses on an important aspect of each retail company: How to set the right incentives to increase the willingness to spend of each customers, while at the same time increasing or at least not decreasing customer loyalty. This can also be transferred to many other problems and industries. Thinking about environmental problems the government could also set certain incentives to decrease pollution. Thus I found the project very interesting and also challenging.

Especially the need to iterate over all events had to be implemented carefully since a lot of logic was involved there.

Furthermore, the feature importance analysis unveiled the importance of feature engineering. Increased efforts in this topic could further increase the final model’s quality.
